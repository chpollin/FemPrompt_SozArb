# Summary: Policy Advice and Best Practices on Bias and Fairness in AI

## Overview
Artificial intelligence deployment in socially sensitive applications has accelerated without corresponding systematic approaches to managing embedded biases. The literature on AI bias and fairness has expanded rapidly but remains fragmented, creating accessibility barriers for researchers and practitioners navigating the field. This paper addresses this gap by providing a comprehensive survey of fair-AI methods, policies, and standards while contributing original guidance through the NoBIAS research project. ## Main Findings

1. **Bias originates from three distinct sources:** pre-existing bias embedded in data (which is non-neutral and value-laden), technical bias from algorithmic design choices and heuristic optimization, and emerging bias from organizational processes deploying AI systems under untested conditions.

2. **Fair-AI research is nascent but rapidly expanding:** Starting only 15 years ago with foundational work by Pedreschi et al. (2008) and Kamiran and Calders (2009), the field has evolved from addressing discrimination in machine learning to encompassing all AI subfields and potential harms to individuals and communities.

3. **Current fair-AI approaches are overly technical:** The dominant paradigm reduces fairness to numeric optimization of metrics while neglecting problem-specific contexts, philosophical frameworks, legal theories of equality, and distributive justice considerations essential for meaningful bias mitigation.

4. **The NoBIAS two-layered architecture bridges legal and technical domains:** A Legal Layer addresses EU regulatory context while a Bias Management Layer systematizes understanding, mitigating, and accounting for bias across intervention levels.

5. **Multiple intervention pathways exist:** Solutions range from non-use of AI systems to comprehensive societal-level interventions, including bias documentation, technical mitigation, auditing, and organizational redesign.

## Relevant Concepts

**Pre-existing Bias:** Bias embedded in training data that reflects historical inequalities and value-laden representations of reality, inherited by AI models during learning.

**Technical Bias:** Bias originating from algorithmic design choices, heuristic optimization strategies, and quantitative loss metrics that oversimplify complex reality.

**Emerging Bias:** Bias arising from organizational processes deploying AI systems under untested or unplanned conditions, reflecting implementation contexts rather than model design.

**Fair-AI:** The multidisciplinary field addressing detection, mitigation, and control of biases in AI-supported decision-making, particularly when biases produce ethically unfair or legally discriminatory outcomes.

**Internal Validity:** The degree to which AI models accurately represent the complexity of reality they abstract, threatened by oversimplification through quantitative optimization.

**External Validity:** The generalizability of AI models across different contexts and conditions, compromised when systems are deployed under untested circumstances.

**Value-Laden Systems:** AI systems that inherently create moral consequences, reinforce or undercut ethical principles, and enable or diminish stakeholder rights and dignity, contrary to claims of neutrality.
