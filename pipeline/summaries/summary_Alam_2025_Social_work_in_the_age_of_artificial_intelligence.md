## Overview

Artificial intelligence systems increasingly mediate critical social work decisions—determining child protective services involvement, housing eligibility, and resource allocation—yet disproportionately harm marginalized populations already experiencing systemic discrimination. ## Theoretical Framework

The analysis integrates three theoretical approaches:

1. **Social Psychology**: Examines how individuals' thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are influenced by actual, imagined, or implied presence of others in AI-mediated contexts.

2. **Group Dynamics Theory**: Analyzes processes within and between professional teams implementing AI systems, including formation, structure, interactions, and performance.

4. **I-C-E Model**: Integrates Ingroup Identification, Cohesion, and Entitativity concepts with socioecological systems theory to understand multidisciplinary implementation teams.

## Key Arguments

**Problem Statement**: Algorithmic systems already determine which families receive child protective services scrutiny, which individuals qualify for housing assistance, and which communities are targeted for intensive policing. These decisions disproportionately affect marginalized populations who have historically experienced discrimination within social service systems. Without proactive engagement from social work professionals grounded in evidence-based practice and human rights principles, AI risks amplifying existing inequities while obscuring them behind a veneer of technological neutrality.

**Core Ethical Tensions**: The analysis identifies how AI integration challenges social work's six core values (service, social justice, dignity and worth of the person, importance of human relationships, integrity, and competence):

- **Human Dignity**: AI systems must preserve clients' inherent worth and avoid reducing individuals to algorithmic categories or risk scores.
- **Self-Determination**: Clients must maintain meaningful control over decisions affecting their lives, even when AI systems provide recommendations.
- **Social Justice**: AI implementations must reduce rather than perpetuate structural inequalities, requiring ongoing evaluation of differential impacts.
- **Human Relationships**: Technology may either augment or undermine the therapeutic alliance central to social work practice.
- **Competence**: Practitioners must develop algorithmic literacy while maintaining relational expertise.
- **Integrity**: Honest communication about AI's limitations and potential biases is essential.
